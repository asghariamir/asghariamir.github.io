Lines & Planes in 3D

Two fundamental objects in three-dimensional space — a line needs a point and a direction, a plane needs a point and a normal. Everything follows from these two ideas.

📏 The Line: Point + Direction
📍

The Core Idea

Pick any point \(\mathbf{a}\) in space. Choose a direction \(\mathbf{d}\). Every point on the line is reached by starting at \(\mathbf{a}\) and walking some distance along \(\mathbf{d}\).

$$\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a} + t\,\mathbf{d}$$
💡

The Parameter \(t\)

When \(t = 0\), you are at \(\mathbf{a}\). When \(t = 1\), you have moved one full copy of \(\mathbf{d}\). Negative \(t\) means walking backwards. The line stretches infinitely in both directions.

🔄

Three Ways to Write the Equation of a Line

Vector form:   \(\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{a} + t\,\mathbf{d}\)

Parametric form:   \(x = a_1 + td_1\),   \(y = a_2 + td_2\),   \(z = a_3 + td_3\)

Cartesian (symmetric) form:   \(\dfrac{x - a_1}{d_1} = \dfrac{y - a_2}{d_2} = \dfrac{z - a_3}{d_3}\)

🔲 The Plane: Point + Normal
📍

The Core Idea

Pick a point \(\mathbf{a}\) on the plane. The normal \(\mathbf{n}\) points straight out of the surface. A point \(\mathbf{r}\) lies on the plane exactly when the vector from \(\mathbf{a}\) to \(\mathbf{r}\) is perpendicular to \(\mathbf{n}\).

$$(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{a}) \cdot \mathbf{n} = 0$$
✖️

Finding the Normal from Three Points

Given three points \(P, Q, R\), form two vectors in the plane: \(\overrightarrow{PQ}\) and \(\overrightarrow{PR}\). Their cross product gives a vector perpendicular to both — that is the normal.

$$\mathbf{n} = \overrightarrow{PQ} \times \overrightarrow{PR}$$
🔄

Two Ways to Write the Equation of a Plane

Vector form:   \(\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} = \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{n}\)   (equivalently, \(\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{n} = d\) where \(d = \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{n}\))

Cartesian form:   \(n_1 x + n_2 y + n_3 z = d\)   where \(d = n_1 a_1 + n_2 a_2 + n_3 a_3\)
