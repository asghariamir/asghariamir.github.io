üìê Essential Theory

Newton's Method

Starting from $x_0$, define

$$x_{n+1} = x_n - \frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}$$

Geometrically: $x_{n+1}$ is where the tangent line at $(x_n, f(x_n))$ crosses the $x$-axis.

Key Idea

Newton is a local linear model. The speed comes from the fact that the model matches the curve very well near a simple root.

Convergence Behaviour

If $x_0$ is sufficiently close to a simple root and $f'(x^*) \neq 0$, Newton's method typically converges very rapidly. In practice, this rapid convergence is often described as quadratic.

Quadratic Convergence (Informal but Precise)

Let $e_n = |x_n - x^*|$ denote the error at step $n$. "Quadratic convergence" means that, once you are close enough to the root,

$$e_{n+1} \approx C \cdot e_n^2$$

for some constant $C > 0$. In practical terms: once Newton is in its stable regime, the number of correct digits roughly doubles at each step.

What This Looks Like in Data

A quick diagnostic is to compute

$$\frac{e_{n+1}}{e_n^2}$$

which should become approximately constant when quadratic convergence has "kicked in".

Failure Modes

Newton's method may fail if:

  • the derivative is zero or very small near the iterate,
  • the initial guess is too far from the root,
  • the function has multiple roots or poor local behaviour.
Key Idea

Newton's method trades guarantee for speed. When it works, it is extremely fast; when it fails, it can fail dramatically.

Problem 3: Quadratic Convergence in Numbers (Core 8)

Continue Problem 2 for one more step to compute $x_3$. Using $x^* = \sqrt{2} \approx 1.41421356$ as a reference:

  1. Compute errors $e_1 = |x_1 - x^*|$, $e_2 = |x_2 - x^*|$, $e_3 = |x_3 - x^*|$.
  2. Compute the ratios $\displaystyle\frac{e_2}{e_1^2}$ and $\displaystyle\frac{e_3}{e_2^2}$.
  3. What do you notice? (This is the numerical fingerprint of quadratic convergence.)
